How I Used Machine Learning to Predict Bitcoin Volatility

Crypto markets are famously volatile. In this project, I set out to answer a simple question: can we build a single index that captures the financial conditions around Bitcoin and use it to anticipate volatility?

Published: 9 Sep 2025 · 10–12 min

Dataset construction

The starting point for any attempt to measure financial conditions is the data. For crypto, that means blending two worlds: the traditional financial system and the blockchain-native one. To do this, I assembled a dataset with 22 variables covering global markets, sentiment, and Bitcoin’s on-chain activity.

On the traditional side, I pulled equity market proxies (S&P 500, NASDAQ, Dow Jones, Russell 2000), commodities (gold, silver, oil), the US dollar index, Treasury yields, and the VIX. Together, these track monetary policy, inflation hedging, and investor risk appetite.

On the crypto side, I included Bitcoin and Ethereum prices, trading volumes, and intraday volatility. To capture fundamentals, I drew from Blockchain.com’s API: hash rate, mining difficulty, block size, transaction fees, and confirmation times. Finally, I added the widely followed Fear & Greed Index as a proxy for investor sentiment.

There were important limitations. Free APIs only allowed me to collect about two years of continuous data (Jan 2023 to Mar 2025). That short horizon means the models risk overfitting to one specific cycle rather than generalizing across regimes. It is one of the most important caveats when interpreting the results.

Before analysis, I applied several adjustments: all price series were converted into daily returns, skewed variables were log-transformed, weekends were dropped, and US market holidays were removed to match trading calendars. Finally, the data was standardized to make it comparable across variables.

From 22 variables to one index

To simplify the complexity, I used Principal Component Analysis (PCA). This method compresses the information into a handful of latent factors. The first six components explained around 70% of total variance, with the first component (PC1) dominated by crypto-native signals like BTC/ETH volumes, volatility, and hash rate. I standardized PC1 into what I call the Financial Conditions Index (FCI).

How the FCI behaves

The FCI rises during stress and co-moves with Bitcoin volatility. In other words, when the crypto market tightens, the index captures it. This pattern validates the intuition that crypto financial conditions can be tracked through a single measure.

Predicting volatility with machine learning

Next, I turned the problem into prediction. I defined high volatility as days where Bitcoin’s volatility exceeded its median, and trained three models—Logistic Regression, Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), and Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA)— using the first six PCs as features. To avoid leakage, the data split respected time order.

Results and caveats

Each model told a slightly different story. Logistic Regression caught nearly all high-volatility cases but produced more false alarms. QDA was conservative, minimizing false positives but missing some events. LDA balanced the two. The ROC-AUC scores were around 0.90, suggesting strong predictive power.

But there is a catch. These near-perfect curves are likely an artifact of the dataset. With only two years of data, the models may be picking up on one particular market regime rather than true universal drivers. Non-stationary variables like hash rate and difficulty, which trend over time, can also inflate separability by acting as hidden time proxies. To harden the analysis, PCA should be fit only on the training set, walk-forward validation should be used, and longer histories incorporated if available.

Takeaways

Despite limitations, the experiment shows that:

  • A crypto-specific FCI can summarize complex conditions into one metric.
  • Machine learning models can turn that index into predictive risk signals.
  • Critical thinking is essential: with short data horizons, results must be treated as upper bounds, not guarantees.

For me, the main lesson is that the combination of macro signals and blockchain fundamentals is powerful. With better data availability, these methods could evolve into real-time tools for portfolio managers navigating the volatility of crypto markets.
