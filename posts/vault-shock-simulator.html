Vault Shock Simulator

This interactive tool stress-tests a DeFi lending vault under a sudden collateral price shock. It models (i) liquidation-driven solvency risk via bad debt and (ii) withdrawal liquidity resilience via a simple liquidity coverage test.

1. Overview and objective

The objective of this model is to assess whether a DeFi lending vault can safely withstand a sudden and adverse price shock affecting one of its accepted collateral assets, without generating bad debt or failing to meet withdrawal requests.

In practice, a sharp drop in collateral prices can stress a vault through multiple channels simultaneously. Even if the underlying asset has passed an ex-ante risk assessment, extreme market conditions may still expose weaknesses in liquidation mechanics, execution liquidity, or balance-sheet resilience.

To capture these dynamics in a transparent and interpretable way, the model decomposes the shock into two distinct but interacting risk channels:

  1. Solvency and liquidations: assessing whether forced liquidations triggered by the price shock can be executed without generating losses for the vault, or whether execution frictions lead to bad debt.
  2. Liquidity and withdrawals: assessing whether the vault can meet a sudden increase in redemptions without gating withdrawals or resorting to disorderly asset sales.

By jointly analyzing these two dimensions, the simulator aims to illustrate how price risk, leverage, and liquidity interact under stress, and how losses or operational disruptions can emerge even in otherwise well-structured lending systems.

2. Vault setup and core variables

We begin by defining the vault’s size and how much of its capital is exposed to the market impacted by the shock. The simulator operates in USD terms for clarity and interprets exposures as fractions of the vault’s total assets.

Vault size (TVL)

The starting point is the vault’s Total Value Locked (TVL), which represents the total value of assets managed by the vault:

  • TVL: total vault assets (USD).

Exposure to the affected market

The vault is assumed to have deployed a fraction of its TVL into a specific lending market (or collateral bucket) that becomes stressed when the collateral price drops. This is captured by:

  • Exposure share: fraction of TVL allocated to the affected market (a number between 0 and 1).

The dollar value of the vault’s exposure to the affected market is therefore:

E = TVL × exposure_share

Shock severity

The stress event is a sudden downward move in the collateral price, focusing on a single shocked collateral market at a time. We express this as a proportional drop:

  • Shock (s): collateral price drop expressed as a fraction (0 to 1). For example, s = 0.35 corresponds to a 35% drop.

3. Borrower leverage, LTV dynamics, and liquidation triggers

In collateralized lending markets, risk is transmitted through borrower leverage. The key quantity is the Loan-to-Value (LTV), which measures how much debt is outstanding relative to the value of posted collateral.

Loan-to-Value (LTV)

By definition:

LTV = Debt / CollateralValue

Intuitively:

  • Higher LTV means the borrower is more leveraged and closer to liquidation.
  • Lower LTV means the borrower has more collateral buffer and is safer.

Liquidation threshold (LLTV)

The market defines a liquidation threshold, often called LLTV in DeFi money markets. If a borrower’s LTV rises above this threshold, the position becomes eligible for liquidation:

  • LLTV: liquidation LTV threshold (0 to 1). Positions above this level may be liquidated.

Why a price shock increases LTV

A key mechanical property of lending markets is that collateral prices can move rapidly, but borrower debt does not automatically decrease at the same time. If collateral prices fall by s, collateral value becomes (1 − s) of what it was. Debt remains unchanged in the instant of the shock, so LTV mechanically rises.

This relationship can be written directly:

LTV_post = LTV_pre / (1 − s)

This explains why leverage risk becomes nonlinear in large shocks: as s increases toward 1, the denominator (1 − s) shrinks and LTV rises rapidly.

Liquidation trigger condition

A borrower becomes liquidatable after the shock if their post-shock LTV exceeds LLTV:

LTV_post ≥ LLTV

Substituting the post-shock relationship gives a simple cutoff in terms of the borrower’s pre-shock LTV:

LTV_pre / (1 − s) ≥ LLTV  →  LTV_pre ≥ LLTV × (1 − s)

This cutoff is central to the simulator. For a given shock size s and liquidation threshold LLTV, the model can determine what fraction of borrowers are pushed into liquidation by checking how many have LTV_pre above LLTV × (1 − s).

4. Endogenous liquidation volume via Monte Carlo simulation

In a live protocol, liquidation volume would be computed directly from the distribution of borrower positions on-chain. In this simulator, borrower positions are approximated statistically in order to focus on mechanisms rather than exact replication.

Modeling borrower leverage

Borrowers’ pre-shock LTVs are modeled using a Beta distribution defined on the interval [0, 1]. This choice reflects the fact that LTVs are naturally bounded and allows the distribution to flexibly capture skew and tail behavior.

  • Average borrower LTV (mean): controls the central level of leverage in the market.
  • LTV dispersion (standard deviation): controls how concentrated or dispersed leverage is.
  • Monte Carlo samples (N): number of simulated borrowers used to approximate the distribution.

Using these inputs, the simulator draws N independent borrower LTVs and evaluates which of them breach the liquidation condition derived in the previous section.

Liquidation share

For a given shock size s and liquidation threshold LLTV, the fraction of borrowers that become liquidatable is computed as:

α(s) = Pr(LTV_pre ≥ LLTV · (1 − s))

This liquidation share α(s) is an endogenous output of the model. It increases nonlinearly with both the severity of the shock and the level of leverage in the borrower population.

Liquidation volume

To translate borrower-level liquidations into a dollar amount, the model assumes equal exposure across borrowers. Under this simplifying assumption, the fraction of liquidated borrowers corresponds to the fraction of exposed capital that must be liquidated.

Q = E · α(s)

where E is the vault’s exposure to the affected market. The resulting quantity Q represents the total notional value of collateral that must be sold under stress.

Why the tail matters

Importantly, the model does not treat liquidations as coming from an average borrower. The simulator separately tracks the average LTV of liquidated borrowers, which is typically much higher than the population average. This ensures that losses are driven by the leveraged tail of the distribution, where risk actually concentrates during stress events.

5. Liquidation proceeds, execution frictions, and bad debt

When liquidations are triggered, collateral must be sold into the market in order to repay outstanding debt. In idealized models, this sale is often assumed to occur at the oracle price. In practice, however, stressed conditions introduce significant execution frictions.

Execution frictions under stress

During sharp market moves, forced liquidations tend to coincide with:

  • Reduced market depth and wider bid–ask spreads
  • Competition among liquidators
  • Liquidation incentives and protocol fees
  • MEV and adverse selection effects

As a result, the price at which collateral can actually be sold is often meaningfully worse than the oracle-implied valuation. The model captures these effects through a single reduced-form parameter: a liquidation haircut.

Endogenous liquidation haircut

The simulator models the haircut as an increasing and convex function of the shock size:

h(s) = h0 + (hmax − h0) · (1 − e−k·s)

where:

  • h0: baseline execution friction under normal conditions
  • hmax: maximum haircut under extreme stress
  • k: sensitivity parameter controlling how quickly execution deteriorates

This functional form reflects the empirical observation that small price moves can be absorbed with limited slippage, while large shocks tend to produce disproportionately worse execution outcomes.

Liquidation proceeds

Given a liquidation volume Q and haircut h(s), the net proceeds obtained from selling collateral are approximated as:

Proceeds = Q · (1 − h(s))

Debt associated with liquidated positions

The debt that must be repaid by liquidations is determined by the leverage of the liquidated borrowers. The model uses the average post-shock LTV of the liquidated subset, ensuring that debt reflects the riskier tail of the borrower distribution:

Debt = Q · LTVliqpost

Bad debt and solvency loss

If liquidation proceeds are insufficient to cover the associated debt, the difference manifests as bad debt borne by the vault:

Bad Debt = max(0, Debt − Proceeds)

To assess materiality, the simulator scales this loss by total vault size:

Loss Share = Bad Debt / TVL

A zero value indicates that liquidations are sufficient to repay debt even under stress. A positive value indicates that execution frictions and leverage have combined to generate insolvency losses for the vault.

6. Withdrawal pressure and vault-level liquidity stress

Even if liquidation dynamics are sufficient to prevent insolvency, a sharp price shock may still trigger a surge in withdrawal requests. Depositors may attempt to exit simultaneously, creating liquidity stress at the vault level. This section evaluates whether the vault can meet redemptions in an orderly manner under such conditions.

Modeling a withdrawal run

Withdrawal pressure is modeled as a fraction of the vault’s total assets requested for redemption over a short horizon:

  • Run size (r): fraction of TVL demanded for withdrawal (e.g., r = 0.20 corresponds to a 20% run).

The total redemption amount implied by the run scenario is therefore:

R = r · TVL

Liquidity buckets

Vault assets are grouped into liquidity buckets based on how quickly they can be converted into cash without incurring losses:

  • L0: immediately available liquidity (idle assets).
  • L1: assets that can be withdrawn or unwound within a short horizon under normal conditions.
  • L2: illiquid or locked positions not available for near-term withdrawals.

Under stress, even assets that are normally withdrawable may be subject to utilization spikes, unwind delays, or adverse pricing. To reflect this, the model applies a discount factor:

  • d: stress discount applied to L1, with values between 0 and 1.

The amount of liquidity realistically available to meet withdrawals is thus:

Lavail = L0 + d · L1

Liquidity coverage condition

The vault is considered able to meet withdrawals without disorderly actions if available liquidity exceeds required redemptions:

L0 + d · L1 ≥ r · TVL

If this condition holds, withdrawals can be serviced without gating or forced asset sales. If it fails, the vault may remain solvent on paper but face operational stress, including delayed withdrawals, emergency unwinds, or realized losses due to rushed liquidations.
